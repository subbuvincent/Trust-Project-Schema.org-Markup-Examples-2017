Our History and Ownership

The Globe and Mail is a part of Canada’s history. We cover events that have changed our nation as well as the day-to-day happenings that continue to shape us as a country. Here’s a quick history of The Globe. George Brown, a reform-minded future Father of Confederation, began publishing a weekly political newspaper called The Globe in 1844. (Schema Tag to be used for year: http://schema.org/foundingDate Format: ISO 8601) In 1880 Brown, then a prominent political figure, was shot in the leg by a disgruntled former employee and died from a resulting infection. (Phillip Crawley has the gun in his office!)

The Jaffray family ran The Globe from 1888 through 1936, when the paper was sold to young financier George McCullagh. When McCullagh acquired The Mail and Empire, that paper was absorbed into The Globe under a new name, The Globe and Mail.

In 1980, Thomson Newspapers acquired The Globe and Mail’s parent company. From 2001 to 2010 The Globe and Mail was part of Bell Globemedia and CTVglobemedia, and today the Thomson family is again the controlling shareholder, via their company Woodbridge.
